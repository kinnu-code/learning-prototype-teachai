# The Science of Memory

Understanding how memory works is one of the most powerful things you can do for your learning. Memory isn't a single thing — it's a collection of processes that work together to help you encode, store, and retrieve information.

## What Is Memory?

<skim>Memory</skim> is the ability to encode, store, and later retrieve information and experiences. Without memory, every moment would feel entirely new — there would be no learning, no identity, no relationships.

Psychologists typically divide memory into three broad processes:

1. **Encoding** — converting sensory input into a form the brain can store
2. **Storage** — maintaining that information over time
3. **Retrieval** — accessing stored information when needed

Each of these stages can succeed or fail independently. You might encode something poorly (not paying attention), store it well, and then retrieve it successfully later. Or you might encode something deeply, but fail to retrieve it due to a lack of the right cues.

## Short-Term vs Long-Term Memory

<skim>Short-term memory</skim> (also called working memory) holds a small amount of information — roughly 7 items, plus or minus 2 — for a brief period, typically seconds to minutes. It's the mental "scratch pad" you use when you're doing arithmetic in your head, or remembering a phone number long enough to dial it.

<skim>Long-term memory</skim>, by contrast, has enormous capacity and can last a lifetime. Information moves from short-term to long-term memory through a process called consolidation, which is heavily influenced by repetition, emotion, and sleep.

> **Key distinction:** Short-term memory is about *capacity and duration*. Long-term memory is about *consolidation and retrieval*.

## The Role of the Hippocampus

<skim>The hippocampus</skim> is a seahorse-shaped structure deep in the brain's temporal lobe. It plays a critical role in forming new long-term memories, particularly for facts and events (known as declarative memory).

Famous evidence comes from patient H.M., who had his hippocampus surgically removed to treat epilepsy. He could still remember events from before the surgery, but could form almost no new long-term memories afterwards — a condition called anterograde amnesia.

| Memory Type | Description | Hippocampus Required? |
|---|---|---|
| Episodic | Personal events ("my first day of school") | Yes |
| Semantic | General facts ("Paris is the capital of France") | Yes |
| Procedural | Skills ("riding a bike") | No |
| Priming | Unconscious influence of prior exposure | No |

## The Forgetting Curve

In 1885, psychologist <skim>Hermann Ebbinghaus</skim> conducted experiments on himself, memorising nonsense syllables and testing his recall over time. He discovered that memory decays in a predictable pattern — rapidly at first, then more slowly.

His <skim>forgetting curve</skim> showed that without any review, about 50% of new information is forgotten within an hour, and up to 70% within 24 hours.

The good news? Each time you revisit information, the forgetting curve resets and becomes shallower. This is the scientific basis for <skim>spaced repetition</skim> — reviewing material at increasing intervals to maximise long-term retention.

## Retrieval Practice and the Testing Effect

One of the most robust findings in memory research is the <skim>testing effect</skim> (also called retrieval practice): actively recalling information strengthens memory more than passively re-reading it.

When you attempt to retrieve something — even if you struggle or fail — you strengthen the memory trace in ways that simply reading the material again does not.

Practical implications:
- Take practice tests rather than re-reading your notes
- Use flashcards with active recall (cover the answer, try to recall it)
- Explain concepts out loud without looking at your notes

## Sleep and Memory Consolidation

<skim>Sleep</skim> is not downtime for the brain — it's when much of the critical work of memory consolidation happens.

During sleep, the hippocampus replays the day's experiences and transfers memories to the neocortex for long-term storage. This process is particularly active during slow-wave (deep) sleep and REM sleep.

Studies show that sleeping after learning significantly improves retention compared to staying awake. Even a short nap can boost memory consolidation.

## Emotion and Memory

<skim>Emotionally charged experiences</skim> are remembered more vividly and reliably than neutral ones. This is because the amygdala — the brain's emotional processing centre — interacts with the hippocampus during memory formation, tagging certain memories as important.

This is why you remember where you were during a major life event, but can't recall what you had for lunch last Tuesday.

In learning contexts, this has a practical implication: connecting new information to something personally meaningful or emotionally engaging makes it far more likely to stick.

## Summary

| Principle | What It Means | Practical Application |
|---|---|---|
| Encoding matters | How you first take in information shapes how well you remember it | Engage actively, don't just read passively |
| Spacing works | Spreading review over time beats cramming | Use spaced repetition systems |
| Testing beats re-reading | Retrieval strengthens memory | Practice recall, not re-exposure |
| Sleep consolidates | Memories are processed during sleep | Don't sacrifice sleep before tests |
| Emotion enhances | Meaningful information sticks better | Connect new learning to what you care about |
